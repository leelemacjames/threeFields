Three Fields

A word that recurs across the sciences is rarely an accident. Sociologists, mathematicians, and physicists each claim it -- and each mean something structurally kindred.

One Word, Three Architectures

The word field does not merely recur by lexical coincidence. In each of its homes it names the same deep pattern: a space equipped with rules of combination that assign relational values to every position within it. The differences matter, but the skeleton is shared.

Pierre Bourdieu's sociological field is a space of positions defined by differential possession of capital. The mathematical field is a set closed under two operations satisfying axioms you likely take for granted. Faraday's physical field assigns measurable values -- scalar, vector, tensor, spinor -- to every point of spacetime. In each case we find the same grammar: an arena, a set of operations, and a structure that emerges from how elements relate rather than from what they intrinsically "are."

But before we can speak of fields, we need to ask a prior question: what kind of numbers are we assigning? Not all measurement scales are equal, and the kind of scale determines which mathematical operations are permissible. This is where the story properly begins.

The Grammar of Measurement

The psychologist Stanley Smith Stevens identified three quantitative number scales, each permitting different operations and different notions of "average." The distinctions are not pedantic -- they determine what it means to compare two measurements at all.

Ordinal

Rank ordering: 1st, 2nd, 3rd... The natural counting numbers. You know that gold beats silver and silver beats bronze, but not by how much. The median is the only meaningful central tendency. Sorting is the characteristic operation.

Interval

Differences are meaningful, but ratios are not, because the origin is arbitrary. Celsius temperature: 20°C is not "twice as hot" as 10°C. Percentage change (a first difference as a fraction of the original) lives here too. The arithmetic mean is the strongest permissible average. The underlying geometry is affine -- only ratios of differences can be expressed.

Ratio

A well-defined origin makes ratios meaningful: one quantity can be twice another. Mass, charge, temperature in Kelvin. The line is closed under division. In addition to the arithmetic mean, the geometric mean (think: root mean square of an AC circuit) and the harmonic mean (think: equivalent conductance of parallel resistors) become valid measures of central tendency.

Perception compresses ratio scales into interval scales

On a greyscale from 0 to 255, our eyes perceive brightness values that increase geometrically -- 2, 4, 8, 16 -- as if they advance in equal steps. The jumps look enormous at the dark end and negligible at the bright end. Our perception applies a logarithm, converting the ratio scale of physical luminance into an interval scale of perceived brightness.

Astronomers discovered the same compression independently. One star is just noticeably brighter than another when its light is about 2.51 times greater. This is the basis of the stellar magnitude scale: a Magnitude 7 star is \(2.51^6 \approx 250\) times as luminous as a Magnitude 1 star, but the magnitudes themselves are spaced as an interval scale -- equal perceptual steps on a logarithmic ruler.

The Affine Space of Interval Measurement

An interval scale, lacking a well-defined origin, inhabits what mathematicians call an affine space. You cannot say "this value is twice that value," only "the difference between A and B is twice the difference between C and D." This is why percentage returns rather than raw prices are the natural objects of study in time series analysis: stock prices live on a ratio scale, but it is the log-prices (or equivalently the percentage returns) that turn out to be stationary -- modelable as draws from a stable probability distribution. The passage from price to return is the passage from ratio to interval, and it is precisely the operation that reveals the underlying statistical regularity.

The ratio scale is the one that demands field structure.
Closure under division is the algebraic field axiom in disguise.

This is the hinge. A ratio scale requires a well-defined zero and the ability to divide any value by any nonzero value and remain in the set. That is precisely the definition of a mathematical field. The measurement theory of Stevens and the abstract algebra of Galois converge on the same structure from opposite directions -- one from the question "what operations does my data permit?" and the other from "what axioms must a number system satisfy?" The rest of this essay traces what happens when that structure is given a spatial address.

Sociological Bourdieu's Field

For Bourdieu, a field (le champ) is a structured social space: the literary field, the academic field, the field of power in which agents occupy positions determined by the volume and composition of their capital. The field is not a container; it is constituted by the relational differences between positions.

Capital comes in species: economic, cultural, social, symbolic. An agent's position is not a single number but a vector -- a multi-component quantity specifying how much of each form of capital they hold. The field has its own "rules of the game" (the nomos), and agents carry dispositions (habitus) shaped by their trajectory through the field.

Key structural features

Relational ontology: positions are defined by differences, not intrinsic properties. No agent's capital has meaning except in relation to the distribution of capital across all other agents.

Autonomy and coupling: each field has its own logic, but fields exert forces on one another. The economic field exerts gravitational pull on the academic field, distorting its internal valuations.

Conservation and exchange: capital can be converted between forms (cultural capital into economic capital, say), but at exchange rates set by the field's own structure.

Mathematical The Algebraic Field

A field in the algebraic sense is a set \(\mathbb{F}\) equipped with two binary operations, addition and multiplication, satisfying a suite of axioms -- closure, associativity, commutativity, distributivity, identity elements, and inverses -- for both operations. What this means concretely: you can add, subtract, multiply, and divide (by anything nonzero) and never leave the set.

$$(\mathbb{F},\,+,\,\cdot\,) \quad\text{such that}\quad \forall\, a \neq 0,\;\exists\, a^{-1}:\; a \cdot a^{-1} = 1$$

The rationals \(\mathbb{Q}\), the reals \(\mathbb{R}\), and the complex numbers \(\mathbb{C}\) are the canonical examples. The integers \(\mathbb{Z}\) fail -- they form only a ring, since most elements lack multiplicative inverses. This is the algebraic expression of what it means for a measurement scale to be a ratio scale in Stevens' sense: the existence of division is precisely the closure under ratios.

Where nature drops axioms

The quaternions \(\mathbb{H}\) sacrifice commutativity: \(ij = k\) but \(ji = -k\). They form a division algebra (every nonzero element still has a multiplicative inverse) but not a field. The octonions \(\mathbb{O}\) drop associativity as well. By Hurwitz's theorem, these are the only normed division algebras over the reals: \(\mathbb{R}, \mathbb{C}, \mathbb{H}, \mathbb{O}\) -- dimensions 1, 2, 4, 8.

Physics seems to care about exactly this sequence. Spinors live in \(\mathbb{C}\) and \(\mathbb{H}\). The exceptional Lie groups, built from \(\mathbb{O}\), surface in string compactifications. The axioms you drop determine the physics you get.

Physical Faraday's Field

Faraday's field is a map from spacetime to values: assign to every point \(x^\mu\) a number, a vector, a tensor, or a spinor. A temperature distribution is a scalar field \(T(x)\). The electromagnetic field is a 2-form \(F_{\mu\nu}(x)\). The metric of general relativity is a symmetric rank-2 tensor field \(g_{\mu\nu}(x)\).

The field concept is not merely descriptive bookkeeping. The electromagnetic field carries energy and momentum; it is dynamical. The Higgs field pervades all of space, and the values it takes (specifically, the fact that it sits in a nonzero vacuum expectation value) give mass to quarks and leptons. A field is not a summary of measurements -- it is the ontological thing doing the interacting.

Fields and measurement scales

Every physical field assigns values drawn from a mathematical field (or a structure built atop one). The temperature field takes values in \(\mathbb{R}\). The electromagnetic potential takes values in a \(U(1)\) fibre -- locally, complex numbers of unit modulus. The connection of general relativity takes values in the Lie algebra of \(GL(4,\mathbb{R})\). The physical field is the mathematical field made spatial.

For the values to be comparable -- for a ratio scale to exist -- we need a well-defined origin and closure under division. Natural units (\(c = \hbar = 1\)) collapse the three kinematical dimensions onto energy, leaving dimensionless coupling constants like the fine-structure constant \(\alpha = e^2/4\pi \approx 1/137\). These are the field values that transcend any human choice of units.

Three Deeper Resonances

The Ratio Scale as Common Ground

Stevens' ratio scale that admits geometric and harmonic means, not just arithmetic requires a well-defined origin and closure under division. This is exactly the algebraic field condition. Physical measurement in ratio-scale quantities (mass, charge, temperature in Kelvin) is possible because the values form a field.

Bourdieu's capital, too, is implicitly ratio-scaled. To say one agent has "twice the cultural capital" of another is to invoke closure under division. The entire machinery of his correspondence analysis, that of plotting agents in capital-space, presupposes that the axes carry ratio-scale meaning. The sociological field inherits the algebraic field's structure whether Bourdieu noticed or not.

The astronomical magnitude scale illustrates this beautifully: our perception is logarithmic, mapping the ratio scale onto an interval scale. The same compression happens in Bourdieu's analysis of taste -- the enormous ratios in economic capital between social classes are experienced as gradual, "natural" distinctions by the agents immersed in the field.

Gauge Freedom and Habitus

In gauge theory, the field values at a point are not directly physical being only gauge-invariant combinations (traces, Wilson loops, field strengths) carrying observable content. You can rotate the internal space at every point independently (a local gauge transformation) without changing any measurement.

Bourdieu's habitus functions analogously. The habitus is the set of dispositions an agent carries -- but it is not directly observable. What is observable is practice: the actions an agent takes. Different dispositions can generate the same practice (just as different gauge choices yield the same physics). The habitus is the gauge degree of freedom of the sociological field.

This is not idle metaphor. In both cases, the unobservable layer (gauge choice, habitus) is the thing that makes the theory local -- it allows you to write down equations of motion (or models of practice) that depend only on the neighbourhood of a point, not on the global state of the field.

The Vacuum and the Doxa

The vacuum in quantum field theory is not nothing. It is the lowest-energy state of the field, and it can have nontrivial structure: the Higgs field's vacuum expectation value is nonzero, and this is what gives particles mass. The vacuum is invisible to agents within the theory precisely because it is everywhere and it is the background against which excitations are defined.

Bourdieu's doxa is the set of presuppositions so deeply shared by all agents in a field that they are never questioned; indeed, never even articulated. The doxa is the sociological vacuum: the background expectation value of belief that makes the field's structure possible but that no agent within the field can easily see.

In both cases, making the vacuum/doxa visible requires an external perspective or a crisis. Spontaneous symmetry breaking in physics reveals the vacuum's structure. A social revolution (or Bourdieu's own sociological analysis) reveals the doxa. The mathematical analogue: the additive identity 0 is the element you never notice until you study the axioms.

The Dimensionless and the Universal

Natural units (\(c = \hbar = 1\)) impose two constraints on three kinematical dimensions, leaving energy as the single free unit. In this system, force has dimensions \(E^2\), distance has dimensions \(1/E\), and the elementary charge \(e\) becomes dimensionless. The fine-structure constant

$$\alpha = \frac{e^2}{4\pi} \approx \frac{1}{137}$$

is then a pure number -- independent of any human measurement convention. It is the field's structure constant in every sense: it measures the strength of coupling between charged matter and the electromagnetic field. An alien civilisation with utterly different units would arrive at the same number.

This is the endpoint of the ratio-scale story. A ratio scale needs a well-defined origin and closure under division -- it needs field structure. Natural units strip away the conventional scaffolding until only the dimensionless coupling constants remain. These are the numbers that characterise the physical field in a way that is, in Stevens' terminology, absolute rather than merely ratio-scaled.

Bourdieu never had dimensionless constants, but the aspiration is the same: to identify the structural invariants of a social field that transcend the particular coordinates (national context, historical period) in which you happen to describe them. The reproduction rate of cultural capital across generations in the academic field might be sociology's fine-structure constant -- if you could measure it with enough precision.

Convergence

The three fields converge on a single insight: structure is relational, not substantial. What an element "is" cannot be stated independently of the operations and relations that bind it to every other element. The number 3 is not a thing in itself; it is a position in the field \(\mathbb{Q}\). A quark is not a thing in itself; it is an excitation pattern in the gauge field. A professor is not a thing in themselves; they are a position in the academic field, defined by differential capital.

Coda: Why the Same Word?

The persistence of the word is not a failure of imagination. It records a genuine structural homology: the pattern of an arena whose elements derive meaning from rules of combination and relative position. The sociological field borrowed from the physical field (Bourdieu acknowledged Faraday and Maxwell explicitly). The physical field, in turn, presupposes the mathematical field for its values to form a ratio scale at all.

The deeper question -- whether there is a single abstract structure of which all three are instances -- belongs to category theory, where a "field" in any of these senses might be formalised as a functor from some structured category to another. That formalisation would be a fourth field. But that is another essay.
